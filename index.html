White hat

Clear comparison of the largest national emitters

Top 15 territorial fossil CO₂ emitters (2022)

Single year · same metric for every country · axis starts at zero

How to read this chart. Each bar shows annual territorial fossil CO₂ emissions in million tonnes, using the same source field for every country. The horizontal axis begins at zero so bar lengths are directly comparable. Darker blue indicates higher absolute emissions. Values are labeled at the end of each bar for precise reading.

Dataset and scope

I use the Our World in Data CO₂ dataset, which compiles territorial fossil carbon dioxide emissions (excluding land-use change) from the Global Carbon Project and related sources. This aligns with the course option on greenhouse-gas-related emissions while keeping a single, well-documented measure. I focus on 2022 and the fifteen countries with the highest national totals that year. Territorial emissions attribute CO₂ to the country where fuel is burned; they do not reflect consumption-based accounting or trade in embedded carbon, which is an important limitation I state here rather than hiding in a footnote.

Design choices for clarity and honesty

I chose a horizontal bar chart because it supports accurate length comparisons for long country names and ranks. The x-axis starts at zero, which is essential for magnitude judgments: truncating the baseline would exaggerate or shrink gaps between countries. Bars are sorted in descending order by emissions so the rank structure matches the visual order. Color encodes magnitude with a sequential blue ramp (lighter to darker), reinforcing amount without implying an unrelated good-or-bad judgment. Tick labels and an axis title explicitly state the unit (million tonnes CO₂) and that the measure is fossil and territorial.

Transparency and limitations

The visualization does not mix per-capita and national totals, does not cherry-pick countries to imply a false ranking, and does not use a second vertical scale that could distort slopes. I document the data lineage in the references section and note that large population contributes to large national totals: readers should consult per-capita or consumption-based views for fairness comparisons across country size. Together, these choices aim to satisfy the white-hat criteria: interpretability for a general audience, explicit transformations (filter: year 2022; top 15 by that year’s total), and visible sourcing and bias context.

Alternatives considered

A world choropleth map would emphasize geography but can distort judgment by area; small countries with high per-capita emissions disappear when encoding totals. A line chart of national totals over decades would show trends but obscure the 2022 cross-section I wanted to highlight for this assignment. The ranked bar chart keeps the task focused: who emitted the most fossil CO₂ in one recent year under a single accounting rule, with minimal embellishment.

Black hat

Parallel trends in U.S. and Chinese emissions?

Emissions paths: China and the United States (2010–2022)

Dual vertical scales · only two countries shown

Surface reading. The two lines occupy similar vertical ranges on the plot, suggesting comparable dynamics across the period. Axis labels identify which scale applies to which country.

Manipulative techniques used

This figure is designed to look like a careful, professional comparison while encouraging a misleading conclusion. First, I apply independent dual y-axes for China (left, orange) and the United States (right, blue). Each scale is stretched to fit only that country’s own minimum and maximum over the selected years. Because both lines are forced to span most of the chart height, their visual slopes appear similarly steep even though the absolute changes are vastly different in million tonnes. This exploits a known pitfall of dual-axis charts: readers often compare line shape rather than reading numbers off two unrelated scales.

Second, I use selective filtering (cherry-picking): I plot only China and the United States and omit rapidly growing emitters such as India, which would complicate a simple “two-superpower” story. The title and framing emphasize “paths” and pairing without stating that the visual similarity is largely an artifact of scaling. Third, the subtitle is vague (“dual vertical scales”) without explaining that the scales are not comparable, which weakens the reader’s guardrails. These choices target at least two rubric categories: misleading encodings / scales and misleading data scope, while keeping a polished aesthetic so the chart still feels trustworthy at a glance.

What a fair reading would require

A reader who extracts numeric values from the axes would see that China’s CO₂ total rises by well over two thousand million tonnes between 2010 and 2022, while the United States declines by a few hundred million tonnes in absolute terms. A single shared scale, or a chart of percentage change from a common baseline with a clear zero reference, or adding other major economies, would tell a less parallel-looking story. The black-hat write-up is intentionally explicit so instructors can verify intent while the standalone graphic remains subtly deceptive for practice viewers.

Why this passes as “subtle”

The graphic uses conventions that appear in legitimate dashboards: color contrast, dot markers, axis titles, and a legend. Nothing is misspelled; grid lines are present. The deception relies on scale choices and omission rather than clownish distortion, so a hurried reader may conclude the two series are “similarly volatile” without checking tick values. That balance matches the assignment’s warning that cartoonish lying is easy to spot, whereas plausible-seeming graphics better train critical reading.

Development process

Work was completed solo. Roughly 6–8 people-hours total: downloading and filtering the OWID CSV and exporting JSON (~1 h), implementing the white-hat bar chart in D3 (~2 h), designing and coding the dual-axis black-hat line chart (~2 h), writing the two long-form sections plus references (~2 h), and GitHub Pages deployment (~0.5 h). The most time went to calibrating the black-hat chart so it looked credible while remaining analyzable in the write-up, and to aligning text with the rubric’s white/black criteria.

AI usage

I leaned on the usual mix of resources while building this. Stack Overflow and random D3 examples got me unstuck on the boring parts—bar chart margins, grid lines with tickSize, hooking up d3.json, that kind of thing. I’d grab a snippet, tweak it until it matched my JSON, and fix whatever broke in the console. Nothing fancy; just normal developer Googling.

I also used Google Gemini pretty regularly. I’d ask it to sketch a first pass at a CSS layout or a chunk of D3 when I was tired of staring at docs, use it to explain an error message, or brainstorm how to word the black-hat section so it sounded analytical instead of preachy. It was genuinely useful for drafting and tightening prose too—I’d take what it gave me, cut stuff that didn’t sound like me, and keep the rest. The ideas for what to visualize (top emitters for white hat, dual axes + cherry-picking for black hat) came from the assignment and class readings; Gemini and Stack Overflow were more like extra hands on the keyboard than the person deciding what the project was about.
